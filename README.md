# HR Analytics - Employee Attrition Analysis

## 📋 Project Overview
This project analyzes employee data to understand key factors driving employee attrition within an organization. Using SQL, Excel, and Power BI, the analysis identifies patterns in job satisfaction, income, work-life balance, and department-wise attrition trends to support data-driven HR decision-making.

## 🎯 Objective
- Identify departments and roles with the highest attrition rates
- Understand the relationship between job satisfaction, income, and employee retention
- Analyze demographic and workplace factors affecting attrition
- Provide actionable insights to help reduce employee turnover

## 📊 Dataset
The dataset contains information on 3,000 employees, including:
- **Demographics**: Age, Gender, Marital Status
- **Job Details**: Department, Job Role, Monthly Income, Years at Company, Years in Current Role
- **Satisfaction Metrics**: Job Satisfaction, Work-Life Balance, Performance Rating
- **Attrition Factors**: OverTime, Distance from Home, Business Travel, Training Times Last Year

## 🛠️ Tools & Technologies Used
- **SQL (MySQL)** - Database creation, data storage, and analytical queries
- **Excel** - Data cleaning, Pivot Tables, and charts
- **Power BI** - Interactive dashboard for visual analysis

## 🔍 Key Analysis Performed (SQL Queries)
1. Total employee count
2. Gender-wise employee distribution
3. Department-wise employee count
4. Average monthly income by department
5. Top 10 highest paid employees
6. Average age of employees
7. Attrition count (employees who left vs. stayed)
8. Employees with more than 5 years at the company
9. Business travel frequency distribution

## 📈 Dashboard
An interactive Power BI dashboard was created featuring:
- KPI Cards (Total Employees, Attrition Count)
- Department-wise Employee Distribution
- Average Monthly Income by Department
- Attrition Analysis by Department/Gender


## 💡 Key Insights
- Employees with low job satisfaction scores (1-2 rating) show significantly higher attrition rates compared to those with high satisfaction (3-4 rating)
- Employees working overtime are more likely to leave the organization, indicating a strong link between overtime and burnout-driven attrition
- Poor work-life balance is a major contributing factor to attrition, especially among employees with less than 2 years at the company
- Employees with lower monthly income relative to their department's average tend to have higher attrition rates
- Newer employees (with 0-1 years at the company) show a higher attrition tendency than long-tenured employees
- The Engineering and IT departments have relatively higher average monthly income compared to Customer Support and HR
- Overall attrition rate across the organization is approximately 14%, which aligns with industry benchmarks for moderate employee turnover
